Project Analysis of "Alien Translator"

By Kelly Xiao (s4002452)

Project beginning

The initial goal of Alien Translator was to create a generative sound tool where a unique sound would play each time the user typed. I wanted users to experience a pleasant and immersive soundscape through simple interaction. As the project evolved, I realised users might feel unsure about what to type, so I introduced prompts to guide them. This shifted the concept toward a conversation with a fictional character—an idea inspired by visual novel games. Key features I aimed to include were sound feedback when typing, volume control, an instruction pop-up for guidance, and branching narrative choices with different endings if there was extra time.

Project values

Besides the novelty of interactive sound feedback, I placed importance on user experience and aesthetic. I recognised the importance of giving users control over the volume and ensuring they were not confused about how to interact with the piece. This led to the addition of a volume button and initial plans for an instruction pop-up. Aesthetics were also a key value, especially given the alien sci-fi context of the project. To enhance immersion, I added floating animations to the character and a typing text animation, improving the overall user experience visually.

Limitations

There were both conceptual and technical limitations that shaped the final form of the project. Due to time constraints, I chose to drop the narrative branching and instruction pop-up, but I did attempt to implement the latter. Technical challenges arose when the pop-up conflicted with the background layers, it would sometimes appear behind the character or leave the black overlay stuck onscreen. With limited time to debug, I ultimately decided to leave it out to maintain the functionality and polish other important elements. As an alternative, I utilised the alien's speech to give users info on what the confirm button does in case users felt lost.

Aesthetic choices

For the project's alien sci-fi aesthetic, it was a spontaneous idea. I thought it would be fun to draw an alien, so i ran with it. The visual style was influenced by a Pinterest board filled with pixel art, colours like blue and purple, and old-school game interfaces. I chose a pixel-like font and designed simple textboxes with light purple borders to match the aesthetic. I also experimented with button shapes, eventually settling on a rounded square design that visually aligned with the textbox style to create a consistent and cohesive look overall.

Creating cohesiveness in the project

To create a cohesive experience across the project, I carefully considered how the visual and sound elements would work together. For the audio, I chose a low, 8-bit sound to complement both the pixel art aesthetic and the alien-themed context of the project. Visually, the layout was kept simple and easy to navigate, with minimal interactive elements to avoid overwhelming the user. A consistent colour palette, like the recurring light purple was applied across textboxes, buttons, and text to maintain a unified and polished look throughout the interface.

Navigating the project

When developing the project, I first prioritised the overall structure of the code so I could build and integrate each element effectively. After setting up the foundation, I focused on creating the image assets, which were time-consuming but crucial for the visual context of the piece. Although I initially wanted to focus on sound design, the visual components took priority due to its importance in showing the project’s atmosphere and tone.
